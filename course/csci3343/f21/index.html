Overview

CSCI 3343 is an advanced undergraduate-level computer vision class covering a wide range of algorithms and applications (week 7-14) with the deep learning (week 4-6) approach. To build up the hands-on experience, We'll have weekly coding labs and one semester-long final project aiming for research publication or real-world deployment.

Course information (aka. How to do well)

1. Meet the prerequisites

  • Maths: We'll cover a wide range of mathematical models in a short amount of time. Knowledge of linear algebra and multivariate calculus is highly encouraged. Please consider taking a linear algebra course before taking this class; otherwise, it may be quite difficult. For a refresher, please see here.
  • Coding: All programming will be completed in Python, using numerical libraries such as numpy, scipy, and PyTorch. The problem sets will be completed using Jupyter notebooks, generally using Google Colab. In some assignments, we'll give you starter code; in others, we'll ask you to write a large amount of code from scratch.

2. Attend the course with preparation

  • 10:00 - 10:50am @ TBD
  • Lectures (MW): Do the reading before the lecture.
  • Labs (F): Bring your laptop.
  • Textbook: We'll be using the following two online books:
    • Szeliski. Computer Vision: Algorithms and Applications, 2nd edition draft (available for free online)
    • Goodfellow, Bengio, Courville. Deep Learning. (available for free online)

3. Ask for help

  • Discord: In addition to the office hours, this course has a Discord server, where you can ask public questions. If you cannot make your post public (e.g., due to revealing problem set solutions), please mark your post private, or come to office hours. Please note, however, that the course staff cannot provide help debugging code, and there is no guarantee that they'll be able to answer last-minute homework questions before the deadline. We also appreciate it when you respond to questions from other students! If you have an important question that you would prefer to discuss over email, you may email the course staff, or you can contact the instructor by email directly.
  • Support: The university counseling services center (CAPS) provides support for a variety of issues, including mental health and stress.

4. Submit assignments in time
Grades will be computed as follows, with all homeworks equally weighted:
Homework 50%
Lab 20%
Final project 30%
  • Homework: There will be homework assignments approximately every week. All programming assignments are to be completed in Python, using the starter code that we provide. Assignments will always be due at midnight (11:59pm) on the due date. The assignments will all be worth approximately equal amounts. You may be asked to annotate your pdf (e.g. by selecting your solution to each problem).
  • Lab: The lab materials are expected to be finished in class. We understand that sometimes you may need more time or have to miss the lab. Thus, the lab deadline is the following Monday 11:59pm.
  • Final project: In lieu of a final exam, we'll have final project. This project will be completed in small groups during the last weeks of the class. The direction for this project is open-ended: you can either choose from a list of project ideas that we distribute, or you can propose a topic of your own. A short project proposal will be due approximately halfway through the course. During the final exam period, you'll turn in a final report and give a short presentation. You may use an ongoing research work for your final project, as long it meets the requirements.
  • Late policy: You'll have 10 late days to use over the course of the semester. Each time you use one, you may submit a homework assignment one day late without penalty. You are allowed to use multiple late days on a single assignment. For example, you can use all of your days at once to turn in one assignment a week late. You do not need to notify us when you use a late day; we'll deduct it automatically. If you run out of late days and still submit late, your assignment will be penalized at a rate of 30% per day. If you edit your assignment after the deadline, this will count as a late submission, and we'll use the revision time as the date of submission (after a short grace period of a few minutes). We will not provide additional late time, except under exceptional circumstances, and for these we'll require documentation (e.g., a doctor's note). Please note that the late days are provided to help you deal with minor setbacks, such as routine illness or injury, paper deadlines, interviews, and computer problems; these do not generally qualify for an additional extension.
  • Academic integrity: While you are encouraged to discuss homework assignments with other students, your programming work must be completed individually. You must also write up your solution on your own. You may not search for solutions online, or to use existing implementations of the algorithms in the assignments.

Acknowledgements: This course draws heavily from MIT's 6.869: Advances in Computer Vision by Antonio Torralba, William Freeman, and Phillip Isola.
